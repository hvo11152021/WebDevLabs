Start your journey with Renewables

Solar, wind, hydroelectric, biomass, and geothermal power can provide energy without the planet-warming effects of fossil fuels.

More than just being green

The growing sector creates jobs, makes electric grids more resilient, expands energy access in developing countries, and helps lower energy bills. All of those factors have contributed to a renewable energy renaissance in recent years, with wind and solar setting new records for electricity generation.

Hydropower

For centuries, people have used river currents for power, making hydropower the largest renewable energy source globally, led by China, Brazil, Canada, the U.S., and Russia. However, large dams can damage ecosystems, displace communities, and face issues like silt buildup, drought vulnerability, and methane emissions. Meanwhile, tidal and wave energy projects offer emerging alternatives with significant potential for clean energy growth.

  • Hydropower is the world’s leading renewable energy source but has environmental and operational drawbacks.
  • Dams can disrupt ecosystems, displace people, and emit methane from decaying reservoir material.
  • Marine energy (tidal and wave) is still small-scale but holds major future potential.

Wind

Wind energy has been used for over 7,000 years and is now a major global power source, led by China, the U.S., and Germany. From 2001 to 2017, global wind capacity grew more than 22-fold to 539,000 megawatts. Despite concerns about appearance, noise, and wildlife safety, wind power—both onshore and offshore—is expanding rapidly as costs decline and technology improves.

  • Global wind capacity rose from 23,900 MW in 2001 to 539,000 MW in 2017.
  • Offshore wind farms are growing, with early leaders in the U.K., Germany, and the U.S.
  • Challenges include visual impact, noise, and risks to birds and bats.

Solar

Solar power is transforming global energy, from rooftop panels to massive solar farms. Between 2007 and 2017, global photovoltaic capacity surged by 4,300%. China, Japan, and the U.S. lead the shift, though solar provided only about 2% of U.S. electricity in 2017. Besides photovoltaics, concentrating solar power (CSP) and solar thermal systems are expanding for electricity, heating, and cooling.

  • Global solar capacity grew 4,300% from 2007 to 2017.
  • China, Japan, and the U.S. are leading the solar expansion.
  • Solar technologies include PV panels, CSP plants, and solar thermal systems.

Biomass

Biomass energy comes from sources like biofuels, wood, biogas, and waste, offering versatile uses for power, heating, and transportation. However, it raises environmental and ethical concerns, such as competition with food crops and the impacts of large-scale biomass trade. Researchers are developing cleaner, more efficient ways to convert waste materials into energy.

  • Biomass includes ethanol, biodiesel, wood waste, and biogas.
  • Concerns include food competition and environmental damage.
  • Innovation focuses on converting waste materials into sustainable energy.

Geothermal

Geothermal energy harnesses the Earth’s internal heat, used for millennia in cooking and heating and now for electricity and building temperature control. It’s a reliable, always-available energy source, though it can release gases like hydrogen sulfide that require careful management.

  • Geothermal energy taps underground heat for power and heating/cooling.
  • It operates continuously, unlike solar or wind.
  • Side effects include hydrogen sulfide emissions with a sulfuric odor.
